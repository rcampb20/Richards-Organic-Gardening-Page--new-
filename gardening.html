Myths

My story

Organic Gardening is a new hobby that I started about two and a half years ago and have become passionate about. My philosophy has always been "we should live a balanced life". I have exercised my entire life, ate fruit and veggies, but have also ate my fair share of junk food.

I have learned so much prior to planting my first seed by doing the dirty work first. I spent hours doing research so it would make the learning process easier when it came to the trial and error phase. There were many things that I learned along the journey that were surprising, and others that were mere myths.

Based on my Experience

There are many myths out there when it comes to gardening. I will try to tackle a few here, but I am sure there will be plenty that I will not address.

Probably the biggest misconception of them all is that you need to use fertilizer when gardening. Stated alternately, feed the plants fertilizer and they will grow. Reality could not be further from the truth. I have been gardening for two and a half years and have never used any on my plants. The quality of the soil is of utmost importance. You cannot just use fertilizer to increase its performance.

You need to be a green thumb to have success in the garden. I have never attempted to plant a seed until I recently started on this journey. Well, maybe when I was a kid in grade school, but that would not have given me any additional knowledge that would have made a substantial difference in the process.

Bugs on your plants are bad and you need to use pesticide to protect them. I have never used any kind of pesticides on my plants.

Mushrooms are bad for your soil and the plants. It is an indication that there is something wrong. I have plenty of mushrooms in my garden and everything is growing fine.


Useful Knowledge

Almost anything grown at home will have a delightful flavor compared to store bought produce, whether it's grown organically or not. It will usually have less chemicals and fertilizer used during the growing cycle.

Most individuals think that USDA Certified Organic mean that the item is free of fertilizer and chemicals. But that is not the case. The growing process is more restricted, and the farmer must follow certain rules. However, some chemicals can be used in limited quantities.

Do not let people dictate what you can grow in certain zones. Test the waters and explorer. Some varieties function better in certain conditions. I have been told I cannot grow certain tropical fruits in my zone. It can be done with persistence and hard work. If I can do it, anyone can!

Feed the soil, not the plants. Provide the soil with organic material each growing season and the insects will do the rest. In the cycle of life, the insects and the micro-organisms will eat the organic matter and their excrement will become fertilizer for the plants. On the biological level, fungus and bacteria have a relationship with the plant's roots and thrive in the decaying material.

Never leave the soil bare for a long duration of time. This will destroy most of the organisms that you have worked so hard to cultivate. Remember, feed the soil, not the plants. Provide the soil with organic material each growing season such as: compost, wood chips, leaves, rock dust, and worm castings.

Benefits

Paradigm Shift

There are countless benefits from organic gardening, and some are not even fully understood at the moment. The important item that makes all these benefits possible is good quality organic soil. With good quality organic soil your food will taste better as well as this plethora of other bonuses that I will list below:

  • Taste – your food will taste better because there will be a higher density of minerals in it, which helps to stimulate your taste buds hence the increased flavor. Fruits are usually sweeter because the sugars are higher when the fruit ripen on the tree. In addition, items do not have to be harvested early to extend shelf life. Therefore, they can stay on the plant/tree until maturity as nature intended. The food is only going from your backyard to the table and not miles across the country.

  • Satisfaction – There is no greater joy than picking food out of your garden that you knew you grew, with your own two hands. It is such a feeling of fulfillment and accomplishment. A feeling you will not get from buying your food at the store. Not to mention the excerise and vitamin D that you get from working in the yard.

  • Chemical and fertilizer use – you might say that makes absolutely no sense. Lets analyze, there is no reason to fertilize with good quality organic soil. The beneficial insect waste in the soil will provide the nutrients for the plants to grow. As long as you provide an environment for them to stick around, you are good! Pesticides are usually used because there is an imbalance in the garden and one species start to take over. If you set up an eco-system to fester the beneficial insect, they will keep each other in check as nature intended.

  • Natural Solutions – another alternative to using pesticides and fertilizer is the use of compost tea. It is a natural solution that is made from several ingredients including compost infused with oxygen for several hours. This enhances the biological activity in the liquid and is used to give the plants additional nutrients. It can also be used if you are having issues with getting your insect population under control. Compost tea can be foliar sprayed onto the plant's leaves to deter insects.

  • Reduces waste – organic gardening reduces waste on multiple fronts. My trash has been cut in half since I started gardening. All my organic waste, like food scraps, are now either composted or goes in the worm bin.

  • Pollution – because of me composting everything and reintroducing it back into the system, there is less methane going into the atmosphere. Methane is a byproduct that is produced from organic garbage when it goes to the landfill, and is not good for the earth. Also, if more people grew some type of food there will be less demand in the supermarket, consequently less pollution from vehicles in the transportation of items to the store.

  • Balanced eco-system – due to the lack of use of pesticides and fertilizers this helps the biological and the beneficial organisms to flourish. Most people are not aware that using fertilizer can destroy your soil. It is like giving your kids steroids to grow instead of feeding them a balanced diet so they can grow gradually and healthy. The micro-organisms in the soil, like mycorrhizal fungi, have a more symbiotic relationship with the plant, and is there for long term gradual healthy growth instead of short term spurts.

    I have never seen so many bugs in my life until I started gardening. I had to learn the difference between the beneficials to the plants and less favorable ones. By keeping a balanced insect population, you use less pesticide. Chemicals not only kills the less favorable insects, but also the good ones, including the microbes in the soil.

Soil

A different perspective of doing things

Mother Nature has done all the work for us. Just observe and mimic the systems she has blessed us with. Soil must have organic matter for plants to thrive. In the forest there is plenty of organic matter which sustains a continuous cycle. The leaves and branches fall to the ground, the insects and organisms digest the organic matter, then their waste fertilize the soil. Some of the organic matter that is not fully digested functions as mulch across the forest floor. This is a never-ending cycle that can be replicated in the garden.

When I first started gardening, I spent weeks gathering woodchips from the dump in preparation of setting up the system. Woodchip is a very useful tool in the garden that helps reduce evaporation and can be used as mulch. It breaks down over time, promoting the growth of a very beneficial fungi called Mycorrhiza. It has a symbiotic association with the roots of plants and is the same process that happens in nature.

There are several other natural items that can be used to enhance the eco-system. Compost which is made from decaying organic matter, can also be spread throughout the garden to increase nutrition. A tea can also be produced from the compost to introduce additional microbes. Rock dust and sea salt can be introduced as food for the micro-organicism. Rock dust and sea salt both have about 90 minerals each as compared to regular fertilizer which has 3 (NPK).

Worm Casting is another way of improving the soil. It is probably the most nutrient and biologically dense item of them all. Worm casting can be produced in a worm bin but the best place to have the worms are in the garden. A good way to tell if you have good organically rich soil is to see if you find a lot of worms in your garden. If you build the environment for them to be happy, they will come!
